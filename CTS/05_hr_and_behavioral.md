## 1. 🎤 Tell Me About Yourself — Formula

> **Structure:** Background → Experience → Skills → Current → Why here

**Template:**
```
"I'm a Python developer with [X] years of experience, primarily focused on 
[your domain: backend development / data pipelines / automation / APIs].

I've worked at [Company] where I [key achievement — specific, measurable].
My core stack is Python, [frameworks like Django/Flask/FastAPI], and SQL,
and I've hands-on experience with [any relevant tech: REST APIs, 
microservices, cloud, etc.].

Most recently, I've been working on [current project] where I [brief contribution].

I'm looking to grow into [area of growth], and Cognizant's work in [digital 
transformation / data / cloud] aligns well with that direction."
```

> **Keep it to 90 seconds.** Practice out loud — not in your head.

## 3. 🔧 Weaknesses — Be Honest + Show Growth

**Good format:** "I used to [weakness]. I recognized this and now I [action taken]. This has helped me [result]."

**Examples:**

- *"I used to underestimate task complexity in estimates. I've since started breaking tasks into smaller units and buffering for unknowns — my estimate accuracy has improved significantly."*

- *"I sometimes get too focused on code quality and perfectionism. I've been practising the 80/20 rule — getting 80% of value from 20% of the effort, and iterating on the rest after release."*

- *"I was less comfortable presenting to senior stakeholders. I've been taking on more demo opportunities in sprint reviews and have grown a lot in that area."*

> ❌ **Never say:** "I work too hard", "I'm a perfectionist" (without context), or something genuinely disqualifying.

## 4. ⭐ STAR Method — Behavioural Questions

Use this structure for **every "Tell me about a time when..." question**:

```
S — Situation:  What was the context?
T — Task:       What was your responsibility?
A — Action:     What specific steps did YOU take?
R — Result:     What was the measurable outcome?
```

---

### Common Behavioural Questions + STAR Templates

#### Q: "Tell me about a challenging project you worked on."
```
S: Our team was tasked with migrating a monolithic Python application to 
   microservices under a 3-month deadline.
T: I was responsible for the core API layer and the database migration strategy.
A: I mapped all service boundaries, designed RESTful APIs with FastAPI, 
   set up database versioning with Alembic, and automated deployments with CI/CD.
R: We delivered on time, reduced API response time by 40%, and the system 
   now handles 3x the original load.
```

#### Q: "Describe a time you disagreed with a team member or your manager."
```
S: During code review, my senior suggested using a different DB schema 
   that I felt would cause performance issues at scale.
T: I needed to respectfully raise my concern without creating conflict.
A: I prepared a small POC demonstrating both approaches' performance with 
   sample data volumes, and presented it in our next sync.
R: The team appreciated the data-backed argument and we adopted a hybrid 
   approach that satisfied both correctness and performance.
```

#### Q: "Tell me about a time you failed or made a mistake."
```
S: Early in my career, I pushed a hotfix directly to production without 
   thorough testing — it broke a payment calculation for 2 hours.
T: I had to own the mistake and fix it quickly.
A: I immediately rolled back, communicated with the team, fixed the issue 
   properly in staging, and established a pre-push checklist for myself.
R: No data was lost. I instituted a personal code review habit before any 
   deployment. Haven't had a similar incident since.
```

#### Q: "How do you handle tight deadlines or pressure?"
```
S: In a previous sprint, two senior developers went on sick leave and I was 
   asked to take on their tasks with the original deadline intact.
T: Deliver 3 people's work as 1 person, on time.
A: I prioritised by business impact, communicated scope risk to the PM, 
   dropped nice-to-haves, and delivered the critical path features.
R: Core features shipped on time. Non-critical items were moved to the next 
   sprint with stakeholder agreement. No crunch required.
```

## 6. 💰 Salary Discussion

> **Golden rule:** Know your market rate. Don't be the first to give a number if you can avoid it.

**Approach:**
1. Research market rate for Python Developer (3+ yrs) in Kolkata / India
2. Have a target number and a minimum acceptable number in mind
3. If pressed: give a **range** (e.g., "Based on my experience and research, I'm looking in the ₹X–₹Y range, depending on the overall package")
4. Don't apologise for your number — state it confidently

**Current market reference (2025):**
- Python Developer, 3 yrs, Kolkata: ₹6–14 LPA (depending on stack, domain, company level)

## 7. ❓ Questions to Ask the Interviewer

> Always have 2–3 questions ready. It shows engagement and genuine interest.

**Good questions:**
- "What does the typical day-to-day look like for a Python developer on this team?"
- "What's the tech stack currently in use, and are there plans to modernise or change it?"
- "How does the team approach code reviews and knowledge sharing?"
- "What would success look like for someone in this role in the first 6 months?"
- "What are the biggest technical challenges the team is working through right now?"

**Avoid:**
- "What does Cognizant do?" (research before you go)
- "How many leave days do I get?" (save for after offer)
